1. Problem Statement and Motivation — The Hidden Cost of Model Selection

Large Language Models (LLMs) have become one of the most widely adopted technologies in both research and industry in recent years. A notable feature of many modern LLMs is the availability of multiple model configurations, particularly the distinction between reasoning and non-reasoning models. While this flexibility is useful, it introduces a practical and underexplored problem: the same query can produce noticeably different outputs depending on which model is used, yet no clear guidance exists for determining which model is the right choice.

The relationship between model capability and output quality is not straightforward. On simple tasks, non-reasoning models often produce more accurate and concise answers with fewer hallucinations, while reasoning models can over-complicate responses when the task does not require deep inference. This suggests that selecting a more powerful model does not necessarily yield better results, and that the appropriateness of a model configuration depends on the nature of the query rather than the capability of the model alone.

This mismatch between model selection and task demands will become increasingly consequential as LLMs grow in complexity. The emerging paradigm for production LLMs is not a single model handling all tasks, but multi-agent architectures (Agentic AI) in which multiple models collaborate, each responsible for the subtask it is best suited to handle. In such systems, model selection becomes a structural requirement rather than a matter of user preference, as assigning a query to the wrong model can introduce errors and inefficiencies that propagate throughout the entire pipeline. Furthermore, each model configuration carries its own systematic biases, meaning that an incorrect routing decision introduces avoidable bias into the output before generation even begins.

Despite this, no widely accepted framework currently exists for automatically determining which model type is most appropriate for a given query. Addressing this gap is both a practical necessity for current deployments and a foundational requirement for the reliable multi-agent AI systems of the future.

2. Related Work — Three Stages of the LLM Pipeline

The LLM pipeline can be broadly divided into three sequential stages: pre-generation, in which a query is received, and a model configuration is selected; generation, in which the selected model produces a response; and post-generation, in which the output is verified or evaluated. Prior work has addressed the latter two stages extensively, but the first — model selection before generation begins — remains largely unaddressed. My research positions itself at that upstream gap. Related work on this pipeline can be understood through two lenses:

  • Academic research, which has produced a body of findings on model behavior, output quality, and reasoning characteristics that this research draws upon to understand the problem space.
  • Industry practice, which reflects how the question of reasoning allocation is being addressed in real-world deployments today.

Together, these two bodies of work establish both what is known about how models behave and what has already been attempted in practice, collectively revealing the gap that this research is positioned to fill.

2.1 Academic Research

Post-generation: factuality and evaluation

On the factuality verification side, DiVA: Fine-grained Factuality Verification with Agentic-Discriminative Verifier (Huang et al., 2026a) addresses the question of "Is the output factually correct?" by proposing an agentic framework that systematically verifies LLM-generated content after generation. DiVA demonstrates that agent-driven verification is an effective strategy for detecting factual errors that surface in LLM outputs. On the model evaluation side, Reasoning Model Is Superior LLM-Judge, Yet Suffers from Biases (Huang et al., 2026b) addresses the question of Is a reasoning model a good evaluator? by examining the capabilities and limitations of reasoning models when used as judges.

While the study confirms the superiority of reasoning models in evaluation tasks, it also reveals that they carry systematic biases that affect judgment quality. Collectively, these studies map out two essential dimensions of output quality: factual correctness and evaluator reliability.

Generation stage: complexity and model behavior

A third relevant line of work examines what happens during generation itself. The Illusion of Thinking: Understanding the Strengths and Limitations of Reasoning Models via the Lens of Problem Complexity (Shojaee et al., 2025) uses controllable puzzle environments to compare reasoning and non-reasoning models across varying difficulty levels. Their studies confirm that a more capable model is not always the better choice: at low complexity, standard models match or outperform reasoning models while using fewer tokens; at medium complexity, reasoning models gain an advantage; and at high complexity, both collapse.

The study also identifies two generation-level failure modes — overthinking on simple tasks and fixation on incorrect solutions at high complexity — that are systematic and not correctable by post-generation verification alone. However, the study is limited to sequential planning puzzles and stops short of any practical prescription. It does not cover factual or open-ended settings, and it does not propose a mechanism for acting on its findings.

Pre-generation stage: the selection decision

This research adds a third dimension to that landscape: the selection decision that precedes generation itself. A methodological bridge toward answering this question comes from Enhancing large language models: alleviating knowledge deficiency with external knowledge and semantically aware reasoning (SAR) (Sornlertlamvanich, 2025), which proposes a dual-process architecture inspired by human cognitive theory, in which System 1 is augmented with structured external knowledge akin to System 2 reasoning. A central mechanism in this architecture is LLM-generated question expansion.

The study demonstrates that the number and quality of generated questions required to adequately represent a piece of content correlate with that content's semantic richness and inferential depth. This suggests a measurable relationship between question generation behaviour and reasoning demand, one that may serve as a lightweight, query-level indicator for automated model selection.

2.2 Industry Approaches

Adaptive thinking within a single model

A related but architecturally distinct approach has emerged at the industry level. Anthropic's adaptive thinking, introduced in the Claude 4.6 model family, allows a single model to dynamically determine whether and how much extended reasoning to apply to each request, rather than relying on a manually configured token budget (Anthropic, 2026). In adaptive mode, the model internally evaluates the incoming request and decides whether to produce a reasoning trace at all, with an external effort parameter providing soft guidance on the aggressiveness of this allocation.

This approach shares the same motivating observation as the present research, namely that not every task benefits equally from extended reasoning, and that allocating reasoning resources uniformly across queries is inefficient. However, the complexity assessment underpinning adaptive thinking is entirely internal to the model: the criteria by which the model judges whether to reason are not formally specified, externally auditable, or independent of the generating model itself. Furthermore, adaptive thinking does not address the selection between categorically different model types, and it does not characterise the systematic biases that each reasoning configuration introduces at the generation level.

2.3 Summary and Findings

Taken together, both bodies of work share the same core observation: different model configurations produce different outputs, and the right choice depends on the nature of the task. Academic research confirms this empirically but stops short of providing a way to make that choice before generation begins. Industry practice has taken a step in that direction, but adaptive thinking works implicitly within a single model, with no formal measure of query complexity, no cross-model scope, and no account of the biases each configuration introduces.

This research fills that gap by combining a structured bias analysis, a query-level complexity measure, and an agentic routing mechanism — providing what neither strand of prior work currently offers: a transparent, bias-aware framework for selecting the right model before generation begins.

3. Proposed Research Plan — Toward Bias-Aware Model Selection

My research addresses the question of "Which model should generate this?", a problem that sits upstream of both verification and evaluation. The goal is to understand the behavioral differences between reasoning and non-reasoning models well enough to inform an automated, bias-aware model selection process. To achieve this, my research plan will proceed in three stages:

Stage 1
Behavioral and Bias Analysis

The first stage systematically compares the output behaviour of reasoning and non-reasoning models across a structured range of tasks, encompassing factors such as semantic complexity, prompt length, task structure, and domain, in order to identify which model selection is better suited to which type of request. Alongside this, the stage conducts bias characterization at the generation level, identifying what types of bias each configuration introduces, such as over-elaboration bias in reasoning models, where extended chain-of-thought produces unnecessary complexity on simple tasks, and positional bias and self-reinforcement bias that emerge from how each model type processes and weights its own prior context. Understanding the nature and conditions of these biases is a necessary precondition for building a reliable selection mechanism.

Stage 2
Query Complexity Estimation

The second stage examines the incoming query itself. Rather than selecting a model based on user preference or task label, this stage investigates whether it is possible to estimate the reasoning complexity of a query from its own properties, such as semantic complexity, syntactic structure, domain specificity, and the number and nature of sub-questions the query naturally decomposes into. This stage develops a query-level complexity signal that can distinguish between requests that benefit from extended reasoning and those that are better served by direct, non-reasoning generation.

Stage 3
Agentic Model Routing

The third stage builds on the behavioural profiles established in Stage 1 and the complexity estimation framework developed in Stage 2, exploring the design of an LLM-based routing agent that automatically analyses a given query and directs it to the most appropriate model configuration between a reasoning and non-reasoning model. The routing agent integrates the findings of the preceding stages into a practical decision mechanism, reducing bias and improving output quality without requiring manual model selection from the user.

Taken together, this three-stage approach moves the research from descriptive analysis through diagnostic measurement toward a practical, deployable framework for adaptive model routing.

4. Expected Contributions and Benefits

As AI systems grow more capable, the future of production deployment is moving toward Agentic AI, where multiple specialized models collaborate to complete complex tasks. In such systems, selecting the wrong model for a given query does not only affect a single response. It can introduce bias that propagates through the entire pipeline, compounding across models and corrupting downstream outputs.

This research investigates the behavioral differences between reasoning and non-reasoning models, the biases each configuration carries, and whether query complexity can be measured reliably enough to automate the selection decision. The goal is a transparent, bias-aware framework that matches each query to the right model before generation begins.
