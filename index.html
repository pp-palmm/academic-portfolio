About Me

Research meets
real-world practice

I hold a Bachelor of Engineering in Computer Engineering with a specialization in Artificial Intelligence from Sirindhorn International Institute of Technology (SIIT), Thammasat University, Thailand. During my studies, I spent five months at Tokyo Institute of Technology, Japan under Professor Tokunaga Takenobu's NLP Lab, investigating the capabilities of large language models in semantic alignment of idea units as part of a summarization evaluation task. One of my research projects at SIIT involved music processing, representing musical pieces using SentencePiece and Word2Vec to classify composers and achieving an F1-score of 1.00 on classical music data, published in Scientific Reports, alongside contributions to IEEE conference papers spanning machine learning and deep learning.

Since graduating, I have spent over 2.5 years in Data and AI Consulting, where I currently lead the Data Strategy Team. My work spans data and AI solution architecture design, data and AI strategy framework development, leading client meetings, data and AI roadmap development, building data pipelines and LLM applications, and working directly with C-level executives on data transformation and product development. Along the way, I've seen firsthand where LLM systems shine in prototypes and exactly where they fall apart in production.

That gap between lab performance and real-world deployment is what draws me back to research. I plan to pursue a Master's degree focused on a specific upstream problem: automated, bias-aware model selection for LLMs. I want to understand how reasoning and non-reasoning model configurations differ in systematic ways, and build a mechanism that routes each query to the right model automatically — so that production AI systems are as reliable and principled as the research behind them.

Research Plan

The right model,
for the right query

Large Language Models now come in two configurations: reasoning models that apply extended chain-of-thought before responding, and non-reasoning models that answer directly. The relationship between model capability and output quality is not straightforward — on simple tasks, non-reasoning models often produce more accurate and concise answers with fewer hallucinations, while reasoning models can over-complicate responses when the task does not require deep inference. Yet no widely accepted framework exists for automatically determining which model type is most appropriate for a given query — a gap that becomes increasingly consequential as LLMs grow into multi-agent architectures where model selection is a structural requirement rather than a matter of user preference.

Stage 1
🔬
Behavioral & Bias Analysis
Systematically compare the output behaviour of reasoning and non-reasoning models across a structured range of tasks — varying semantic complexity, prompt length, task structure, and domain — to identify which configuration is better suited to which type of request. The stage also characterizes generation-level biases: over-elaboration bias in reasoning models, where extended chain-of-thought produces unnecessary complexity on simple tasks, and positional bias and self-reinforcement bias arising from how each model type processes its own prior context.
▶
Stage 2
📐
Query Complexity Estimation
Rather than selecting a model based on user preference or task label, this stage investigates whether reasoning complexity can be estimated from the query's own properties — its semantic complexity, syntactic structure, domain specificity, and the sub-questions it naturally decomposes into. The result is a query-level complexity signal that distinguishes requests benefiting from extended reasoning from those better served by direct, non-reasoning generation.
▶
Stage 3
🔀
Agentic Model Routing
Building on the behavioural profiles from Stage 1 and the complexity estimation framework from Stage 2, this stage explores the design of an LLM-based routing agent that automatically analyses a given query and directs it to the most appropriate model configuration. The routing agent integrates the findings of the preceding stages into a practical decision mechanism, reducing bias and improving output quality without requiring manual model selection from the user.

Why this matters: Academic research confirms that model configurations produce systematically different outputs depending on task complexity, but stops short of providing a way to make that choice before generation begins. Industry practice has moved in this direction — but without a formal measure of query complexity, no cross-model scope, and no account of the biases each configuration introduces. This research fills that gap by combining a structured bias analysis, a query-level complexity measure, and an agentic routing mechanism — reducing unnecessary reasoning overhead on simple tasks, preventing underperformance on complex ones, and making model configuration decisions principled rather than based on user intuition.
